### Monday, May 4 — LinkedIn Article

**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Post Type:** Article (Long-Form)

**Title:** Questions Every Executive Chef Should Ask Before Choosing a Food Supplier

**Article Copy:**
---
In over a decade of supplying premium hotels and restaurants across Phuket, we've sat across the table from hundreds of F&B managers and executive chefs. The best conversations — the ones that lead to genuine, long-term partnerships — always start with the right questions.

Not about price. Not about minimum order quantities. The real questions.

Here are the ones we believe every chef and F&B professional should be asking any food supplier before signing on the dotted line:

**1. Where does this product actually come from?**

Provenance matters. Not just for quality — for consistency. A supplier who can't tell you the origin of their product, the producer's name, or the country of manufacture is a supplier working from a black box. At Really Good Deli, every product in our range has a documented origin story. Because if we can't tell you where it comes from, how can we guarantee it'll be the same next month?

**2. What happens when something goes wrong?**

Delivery delays. Damaged stock. The wrong product arrives on a Friday before a full weekend of covers. It happens. What matters is how your supplier handles it. Do they have a direct contact you can reach at 7am? Do they replace stock immediately, or do you chase invoices for three weeks? Ask your supplier about their failure response process before you need it.

**3. How do you handle seasonal availability?**

Many premium deli products — charcuterie, aged cheeses, specialty condiments — are subject to seasonal supply fluctuations. A great supplier plans ahead, communicates early, and offers alternatives when supply tightens. A poor supplier goes quiet and leaves you scrambling to revise your menu the week before service.

**4. Can you grow with us?**

As your menu evolves, your supplier needs to evolve with it. The right partner isn't just filling today's orders — they're paying attention to where your kitchen is heading, suggesting new products, and helping you stay ahead of guest expectations.

**5. Who else in Phuket are you supplying?**

This one matters. Not because exclusivity is always possible or even necessary, but because a supplier who works with your direct competitor across the road has a conflict of interest worth understanding. It's a fair question — and a credible supplier will answer it honestly.

At Really Good Deli, we supply 40+ four and five-star properties across Phuket. We believe in transparency, long-term partnerships, and the kind of service that makes your kitchen run smoothly — even on the hard days.

If you're evaluating your current supplier or looking to add a reliable deli partner to your roster, we'd welcome the conversation.

### Monday, May 18 — LinkedIn Article

**Platform:** LinkedIn
**Post Type:** Article (Long-Form)
**Pillar:** Thought Leadership

**Title:** Sourcing with Intention: What Sustainability Really Means for a Specialty Food Supplier

**Article Copy:**
---
"Sustainable" has become one of the most overused words in the food industry. It appears on packaging, in brand decks, and in press releases — often without a clear definition of what it actually means in practice.

We've been thinking about this at Really Good Deli for a while. And we want to share how we think about it — not as a marketing position, but as an operating principle.

**Supply Chain Transparency**

Sustainability, for us, starts with knowing where things come from. Not just the country of origin — but the producer, their methods, and how the product travels to reach us. Short supply chains aren't just better for quality. They're better for accountability. When we can trace a product back to a specific producer, we can make informed decisions about quality, consistency, and ethics.

**Supporting Regional Producers**

Where possible, we source regionally. Products that can be produced closer to Thailand — whether in Southeast Asia or through regional importers with strong producer relationships — reduce transport time, reduce handling, and support producers in our extended region.

This isn't always possible for every product in the range, and we won't pretend otherwise. Some of the world's best charcuterie comes from Europe. Some cheeses are simply not replicable elsewhere. But the principle — regional where we can, transparent always — guides our sourcing.

**Reducing Waste in the Supply Chain**

Food waste at the distribution level is a real problem in hospitality. Oversupply, short shelf-life windows, and poor demand forecasting lead to enormous waste across the industry.

We work with our hotel and restaurant partners on demand planning — reviewing order histories, flagging seasonal trends, and helping F&B teams order smarter rather than ordering more. The result is less waste, lower costs, and a supply chain that runs leaner.

**The Long View**

We've been operating in Phuket for over a decade. We're not here to extract value from the market — we're here to be part of it. That means investing in relationships, being honest about limitations, and building a business that our partners can rely on for the next ten years, not just the next season.

Sustainability, at its core, is about longevity. The kind you earn through consistent, responsible practice.

If you'd like to discuss our sourcing and supply practices in more detail — for procurement reviews, tender documentation, or simply out of interest — we're always happy to talk.

**Title:** The State of the Hospitality Supply Chain in Thailand: What F&B Leaders Need to Know in 2026

**Article Copy:**
---
After a decade of supplying premium hotels and restaurants across Phuket, we've had a front-row seat to significant shifts in how Thailand's hospitality supply chain operates. Some of these changes were gradual. Some were forced by circumstance. All of them have material implications for how F&B leaders plan, purchase, and partner.

Here is our honest assessment of where things stand in 2026.

**1. The Post-Pandemic Supply Chain Still Hasn't Fully Stabilised**

The disruptions of 2020–2022 exposed deep fragilities in global food supply chains. For Thailand's hospitality sector — heavily dependent on imported specialty ingredients — the impact was significant. Port delays, air freight surcharges, and producer-level disruptions drove costs up and predictability down.

In 2026, the situation has improved, but it hasn't normalised. Lead times for European specialty products remain longer than pre-2020 benchmarks. Several producers who were temporarily unavailable haven't returned to market. And the cost base has shifted permanently upward in many categories.

F&B leaders who haven't revised their procurement strategy since 2019 are operating with outdated assumptions.

**2. The Case for Fewer, Deeper Supplier Relationships**

One trend we've observed across our client base is a move away from fragmented multi-vendor supply toward consolidated, deeper relationships with fewer partners. The logic is sound: fewer vendors means less administrative overhead, more buying leverage, and critically — better communication when issues arise.

Hotels that work with three to four key suppliers, rather than fifteen, consistently report better supply reliability and less time spent on vendor management. The trade-off is dependency risk — which is why the quality of each partnership matters more than ever.

**3. Local and Regional Sourcing Is Growing**

The availability of high-quality, locally and regionally produced specialty foods has improved considerably in the past five years. Thai producers, Southeast Asian artisans, and regional importers with strong producer relationships are now viable alternatives to purely European-sourced supply in several categories.

This matters for cost, reliability, and — increasingly — for the story on the menu. Guests, particularly at the upper end of the market, are interested in provenance. A cheese from [regional producer] or a charcuterie item from [local producer] has a story that a commodity product from a distribution warehouse cannot match.

**4. Technology Is Lagging in Thai F&B Procurement**

Most F&B procurement in Thailand's hotel sector is still managed through email, spreadsheets, and WhatsApp. Compared to markets like Singapore or Australia, the adoption of procurement management tools is significantly behind. This creates inefficiency, increases the risk of ordering errors, and makes demand planning difficult.

We're not in the software business, but we'd encourage F&B leaders to explore the tools available — even basic ones — to bring more structure to their supplier management.

**5. The Opportunity for Premium Positioning**

Despite cost pressures, demand for premium, differentiated F&B offerings at Phuket's top hotels remains strong. Guests are spending. The properties competing on experience — not just rate — are performing. And the supply chain that supports premium positioning is a competitive advantage, not a cost centre.

The F&B teams that understand this are investing in supply partnerships that enable the menu, not just fill it.

Twelve weeks ago, we launched Really Good Deli's formal presence on LinkedIn, Instagram, and Facebook — and started the process of building an online retail channel alongside our existing wholesale business.

It's been a busy quarter. And we've learned a lot.

Here's an honest look at what those three months have taught us — about digital marketing, about our industry, and about the Phuket hospitality community.

One of the things that surprised us most was the response from the Phuket hospitality community — chefs, F&B managers, hotel operators, and industry peers who reached out, connected, and shared our content without being asked.

The hospitality industry in Phuket is tight-knit. Word travels. Relationships matter. We always knew that on the ground — but seeing it play out digitally, in real time, has been genuinely encouraging.

**2. B2B and B2C Are Different Languages**

Building content for both wholesale clients and retail consumers simultaneously has been an interesting exercise. The audiences want different things. The tone needs to shift. The call-to-action is different.

What we've found is that the underlying brand story — quality, provenance, consistency, care — resonates with both. The delivery just needs to be calibrated. A chef cares about reliability and product performance. A retail customer cares about experience and story. Both care about quality.

**3. Thought Leadership Is Real**

We'll be honest: we weren't sure how much a specialty food supplier talking about industry trends on LinkedIn would resonate. The answer, based on three months of data: more than we expected.

Content about supply chain dynamics, sourcing practices, and practical advice for F&B professionals consistently outperformed purely promotional posts. People engage with useful content. They share it. They save it.

The lesson: be genuinely useful before being promotional.

**4. Relationships Close Business**

Of the new client enquiries we received in Q1, the vast majority came through personal referrals or direct outreach — not from our paid media or organic social reach. This confirms what we already knew: in this industry, relationships close business.

Digital marketing's role, for us, is to support and extend those relationships — not replace them.
